Worm Health Reference

Everything you need to grow a thriving colony fast.

🎯 Ideal Conditions

Parameter Optimal Range Danger Zone Notes
Temperature 55 – 77°F Below 40°F / Above 85°F Peak reproduction at ~70°F. Will die quickly above 95°F.
pH 6.0 – 7.0 Below 5.5 / Above 8.0 Add crushed eggshells to raise pH; avoid citrus which acidifies fast.
Moisture 70 – 85% Below 60% / Above 90% Bedding should feel like a wrung-out sponge. Never standing water.
Bedding Depth 6 – 12 inches Less than 4 inches Deeper bed = more stable temps and more real estate for the colony.
Feeding Rate ½ weight/day max Overfeeding causes rot & gas 1 lb of worms can process ~½ lb of food per day at peak. Start slow.
Population Density ~1 lb per sq ft Over 2 lbs per sq ft Overcrowding slows reproduction and stresses the colony.

✅ Good Foods

  • Fruit & vegetable scraps (non-citrus)
  • Coffee grounds & paper filters
  • Tea bags (remove staples)
  • Crushed eggshells (pH buffer)
  • Shredded cardboard & newspaper
  • Bread & grains (small amounts)
  • Aged manure (horse, cow, rabbit)
  • Leaves & grass clippings (no pesticides)

🚫 Avoid These

  • Citrus peels — acidic, drives worms away
  • Onions & garlic — toxic in large amounts
  • Meat, fish, dairy — attracts pests, causes rot
  • Oily / spicy foods
  • Pet waste — disease risk
  • Glossy paper or cardboard
  • Fresh grass clippings in bulk — heats up
  • Any treated/chemically sprayed plant matter

🚨 Escape Troubleshooting

Too Acidic
Add crushed eggshells or a pinch of garden lime. Check pH — below 6.0 is common when citrus or large amounts of fruit are added.
Too Wet / Anaerobic
Add dry shredded cardboard and fluff the bedding. Ensure drainage holes in the bottom. Worms flee when oxygen drops.
Too Hot
Move the bin to a cooler location. Basement or garage (not uninsulated in summer) is ideal. Add frozen water bottles to cool quickly.
New Bin Stress
Normal for 1–2 weeks. Leave a lamp shining above the bin overnight — worms instinctively retreat from light.
Overfeeding
Wait until previous food is mostly gone. Rotting uneaten food creates gases (ammonia, H₂S) that repel worms.
Vibration / Disturbance
Keep the bin away from washing machines, loud music, or frequent foot traffic. Vibrations mimic predators.

🪱 Common Composting Worms

Red Wigglers ⭐
Eisenia fetida
The gold standard for home composting. Thrives in organic-rich surface layers, reproduces fast, tolerates a wide range of conditions. Ideal for totes.
European Nightcrawlers
Eisenia hortensis
Larger than red wigglers. Great compost producers and excellent fishing bait. Slightly cooler temperature preference (50–70°F).
African Nightcrawlers
Eudrilus eugeniae
Very fast composters in warm climates. Need temps above 60°F and prefer 70–80°F. Sensitive to cold — not ideal for unheated spaces.
Canadian Nightcrawlers
Lumbricus terrestris
Burrowing worms — not ideal for tote composting. They need cool, deep soil (40–60°F). Better for garden beds than bins.

📈 Maximize Population Growth

Temperature Sweet Spot
Keep the bin at 70–75°F. Red wigglers can double their population in 60–90 days under ideal conditions. Every degree below 60°F slows reproduction noticeably.
Feed Consistently
Small, frequent feedings beat large dumps. Bury food in different spots each time — this distributes the colony and prevents anaerobic hot spots.
Darkness Is Key
Keep the lid on and the bin in a dark location. Worms that aren't stressed by light stay focused on eating and reproducing.
Harvest Castings Regularly
Once castings build up (dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling), migrate worms to fresh bedding. Too many castings inhibit reproduction.
Protein Boost
A small amount of cornmeal, oatmeal, or chicken mash mixed into bedding can accelerate breeding cycles. Use sparingly — overuse causes overheating.
Ventilation Without Drying
Drill ¼" holes in the lid and upper sides. Lay damp burlap or cardboard directly on bedding surface to retain moisture while allowing gas exchange.
